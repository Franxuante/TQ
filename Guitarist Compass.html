I created this chart on a whim. It's based my own aesthetics and surely very
biased. If you are curious about my criteria, you can check out Piero Scaruffi's
website scaruffi.com. Once a friend told me that my way to approach aesthetics
in music is quite similar to Scaruffi’s. I checked his website, and I actually agreed
to at least 60% of his stuff. The major difference is that I’m more technical driven
in assessing rock music, or at least guitar players. Scaruffi also published a list of
best rock guitarists of all time https://www.scaruffi.com/music/guitar.html. I
found some parts quite intriguing. Yet it seemed that Scaruffi, with a deeper
root in jazz and classics, hadn't paid enough attention to rock guitar music,
especially the footage of some rare live performances (There’s no list of all-time
best rock performances on his website. It’s understandable since the internet
didn’t prevail in the last century.).

Brief interpretation of each quadrant:
Up: Graceful

1


Down: Cringe
Left: slow hands. Since some players are more rhythm-driven, this could be
also be righteously misinterpreted as non-shredders.
Right: shredders

Red Quadrant: LESS IS MORE, ingenuity, artsy.
Blue Quadrants: MORE IS MORE, virtuosity, craftsmanship, discipline.
Green Quadrant: LESS IS LESS, boring or simply unqualified as a professional
(harsh).
Purple Quadrant: MORE IS LESS, bullshitting, some not aware of their
tawdriness, some just technocrats.

A player’s influence or achievements is not a major criterion to assess his level of
grace. Instead, it largely comes from a purely aesthetic perspective. It is
definitely possible for a cringe player to exert a major influence on a graceful
player. Wise players can synthesise and refine what they were fed with. If you
tend to assess an artist by the sales of their artworks or popularity, my website
might be a hostile place for you.

Due to the limitation of space, many other guitarists that one idolises or loathes
might not be included. Some excellent guitar players like Dave Mustaine, James
Hetfield, and Billy Corgan are mainly in charge of rhythm and songwriting in
their groups. I’d rather not include them in this chart, since it primarily aims to
assess lead players or guitarists with a regular solo career. It will be better to
evaluate those rhythm and songwriting geniuses with another chart. On the
perfect centrist point lie Page and SRV, whose names are blurred by the axis.

Don take this chart seriously, peace.

Appendix I: Brief Remarks on Each Player from the Chart by
Quadrant

Red Quadrant

Jimi Hendrix
The GOAT of electric guitar. A lofty concept for every guitar player.
In addition to reinventing blues and rock, he might be the only one here who can translate any
image, senses, or hallucinations into licks. Technically speaking, I am more drawn to his

2


rhythms than his coarse yet spiritually transcendental leads — or maybe they’re inseparable.
He blended chords and melodies to unlock the piano function of guitar.

Prince
The GOAT of pop. A godly man who plunged the concept of ‘entertainment’ into an
unfathomable depth and a versatile maestro who levitated pop music to an unprecedented
altitude. He had the best phrasing I have ever seen. Adding or subtracting any note from any
of his licks would diminish its musicality. His solo over ‘Something in the Water’ (sound check
in Miami, 1985) is my all-time favourite.

John Frusciante
A water down of every pre-90s rock sage and a trailblazer during the 00s. The ultimate bundle
of new wave, punk, funk, blues rock, hard rock, alt rock, art rock, prog rock, avant music, and
IDM. My all-time greatest influence. SEE MY 4700-WORD BLOG ON HIM.

Rory Gallagher
Criminally underrated blues master admired by Hendrix. He merged Irish folk music into
blues, which diversified his note choices and rendered a fresher style. His hexatonics
outplayed others’ pentatonics or blues scales. He can instigate chaos and mayhem with a few
slightly overdriven notes. His life is a miniature of musical dedication and tragic heroism.

Frank Zappa
Anti-establishment, anti-boredom, counter-counterculture warrior and intellectual. With
Captain Beefheart, he experimented and realised Stravinsky’s avant ideas on guitar. The
perennial game changer. The GOAT of rock composition.

Eddie Hazel
The OG of groove in rock guitar. He narrated African (American) myths and lores through his
groove. He initiated psychedelic funk. Maggot Brain is a monument in guitar history.

Peter Green:
As criminally underrated as Rory, he made blues exotic. The one who knows where to stop the
best. A white (or Jewish) B. B. King.

Syd Barrett:
Prog rock started here. The very first to make ‘emo’ music with an electric guitar. He
concocted a grandiose, startling, yet appalling scene of mental havoc on six strings. Only
Frusciante’s Niandra Lades had come close.

Roy Buchanan:
He has a unique interpretation of the blues classics. So bluesy that I almost felt blue blood
running in his veins.

Tom Verlaine
With Richard Lloyd, he defined guitar virtuosity in new wave rock by maximising the use of
diatonic and modal scales. He delivered magnificence through nonchalant playing. I can hear
iron melting and oxygen frosting in his delicate tone.

Terje Rypdal
Scandinavian comfort in jazz fusion.

Among the first few to make non-guitar music with a guitar. He really pushed the boundaries.

Robert Fripp
It's incredible that this dude can write alien riffs and exquisite yet accessible solos with
deliberate treatment of the intervals.

Omar Rodriguez
An anti-guitar hero who’s actually very technical to navigate hostile meters change and
complex arpeggios. One of the most avant and alternative guys in this century.

Hillel Slovak
A sultry storm. He made history by mingling hard rock, funk, and psychedelic rock. Godfather
of heavy funk. Ephemeral nameless hero.

Trey Anastasio
A bridge between 70s fusion and 90s alt/indie wave. He showed people that jam band rules.

Jeff Beck
Epitome of ‘less is more’ with his magic thumb and whammy.

Lou Reed
He did way more than he could with limited technical proficiency. He knew how to behave
himself, given that he was a noob. Besides, he invented new wave.

Sonny Sharrock
Mystical wizard of jazz fusion, folk, and psychedelia.

Tony Iommi
HEAVY METAL BEGAN HERE! What made him a bit low here is his limit in melodic phrasing.

Larry Carlton
A crucial figure for 70s fusion/rnb guitar. Larry stands for sweetness and humour.

J Mascis
It's unbelievable that he created the alt/indie vibe with a crunchy, fuzzy tone when nobody

David Gilmour:

Blue Quadrant

Allan Holdsworth
Some call him the Coltrane or Messiaen of the guitar, others the Euclid of guitar-oriented
music theory. Yet such titles barely graze the facade of his genius. The only legit verdict is:
ALIEN ONLY VISITS EARTH ONCE.

John McLaughlin
No matter in the Mahavishna Orchestra or in his solo career, he demonstrated his virtuosity in
almost every genre. He could emulate Hendrixy blues licks as well as a gypsy jazz solo. He’s
more avant than most ‘avant’ or ‘prog’ rock guitarists.

Shawn Lane
As a humble learner of Indian traditions, he became the most technical guitarist in history. No
one else can play 18 notes in one second while maintaining melodic smoothness. Unlike other
aggressive shredders, he always managed to express his inner peace through exuberant
phrases. A combination of Holdsworth and Hendrix.

5



Jason Becker
It's a big shame that he’s consigned by many to oblivion due to his tragedy. That Jason caught
ALS was the biggest loss in metal history. Jason is the GOAT of sweep picking. No one ever
close. His harmonies (especially with echo/delay effect) showcased his terrific grasp of
classical composition and rhythmic accuracy. Together with Marty, he startled the guitar
community with their miracles in Cacophony.

Steve Vai
If Hendrix and Zappa had a child who could shred effortlessly. This guy really played it cool!
Also the one who got the most out of Zappa.

Marty Friedman
A humble magician who orchestrated grotesquely exotic elements in heavy metal. Incredible
timing. Always seems to be out of tempo but always in the groove.

Paul Masvidal
With Jason Gobel, he permanently changed the way to play metal guitar, which propagates to
present (the solo of ‘Cosmos’ set the new rules). He was the first to bring Holdsworth’s
otherworldly ideas to metal. Through intricate, revolutionary arrangements, he pondered
abstruse philosophical questions such as existentialism and self-acceptance. Focus is my
all-time favourite metal album!

Pat Metheney
Best swing in rock, best rock in swing.

Andy Timmons
The ultimate controller of DYNAMICS and melodies. Shredding with overflowing emotions.

Gary Moore
Blues. Shredding. Expressions. The only successor of Rory and Peter.

Eric Johnson
He invented the creamy, smooth fuzzy lead tone for lavish super-pentatonic runs and the
crystal, nitid rhythm tone for euphoric chord progressions. It’s always a sunny, breezy spring
day to listen to his music.

Frank Gambale
Another shredding fusion pro who appeared in ‘Truth in Shredding’ with Holdsworth and in
‘Centrifugal Funk’ with Lane. His distinct treatment of the 4ths distinguished himself from
other fusion players.

Buckethead
Out of the kill-switch-manoeuvred 80s hard rock grooves, Lane-Gilbert-style legatos, John
Zorn’s free jazz anarchy, P-Funk-infused occult, and Primus-inspired circus eccentricities,
Brian Carroll forged BUCKETHEAD. Buckethead defines an uneducated nobleman. Buckethead
is beyond a figure. As long as anyone in that mask starts to play guitar, Buckethead surfaces
from underground. It is a parody of mass hypnosis. It is an invasion against the rebel-wannabe
mainstream who intrinsically conforms. It is B for Bendetta.

Alexi Laiho

John Petrucci

Guthrie Govan

Oshio, or the corniest Marcin (acoustic version of Tim Henson). Centurial electric shredders
like EVH made a big difference. No matter how flashy the electric guitar is, it still serves as a
track for the entire song. However, acoustic acrobats make naive attempts to replace the
ensemble with some skateboard tricks on guitar. John Fahey didn’t try to replace the drum by
frolicking with the body or pick-guard. Instead, he reached the electric-equivalent effects
with candid, austere fingerpicking. He was the first to make ambient music with an acoustic
guitar.
